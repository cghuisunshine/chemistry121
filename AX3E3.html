Valence Bond Theory: Why No AX3E3?

Question: Why is there no stable AX3E3 geometry when we look at it using Valence Bond Theory (VBT)?

1. What Does AX3E3 Mean in VBT?

In the AXnEm notation:

  • A = central atom
  • X3 = 3 bonding pairs (3 σ bonds)
  • E3 = 3 lone pairs

Total electron domains = 3 (bonds) + 3 (lone pairs) = 6.
With 6 domains, the parent electron-domain geometry would be octahedral.

So in Valence Bond Theory, the central atom must use six hybrid orbitals arranged in an octahedral pattern.

2. Octahedral Geometry and sp³d² Hybridization

In VBT, an octahedral arrangement is described using sp³d² hybridization:

  • The central atom mixes 1 s, 3 p, and 2 d orbitals → 6 equivalent sp³d² hybrids.
  • Each sp³d² hybrid orbital points toward one of the six octahedral directions.

These six hybrids must be occupied by:

  • 3 bonding pairs (for the three A–X σ-bonds), and
  • 3 lone pairs (E3).

That means all six sp³d² orbitals are filled, some with bonds, some with lone pairs.

3. Where Do Lone Pairs Prefer to Go in VBT?

In Valence Bond Theory, lone pairs usually:

  • Prefer orbitals that are more p-like and diffuse, and
  • Prefer orientations where they are not pointing directly at other electron domains (to reduce repulsion).

However, in an sp³d² set:

  • All six hybrid orbitals are equivalent (same energy, same s/p/d mix).
  • All of them point directly along the six octahedral directions.

There is no special “safe” orbital for lone pairs to hide in. Every hybrid is pointing straight at some direction in space, which also corresponds to where ligands would like to be.

4. Trying to Place 3 Bonds and 3 Lone Pairs

For AX3E3, we would try to:

  • Put 3 bonding pairs into three sp³d² hybrids (for 3 A–X σ-bonds).
  • Put 3 lone pairs into the remaining three sp³d² hybrids.

But in an octahedral arrangement, each orbital has 90° relationships to four others. So:

  • Each lone pair sits 90° away from several bonds and at least one other lone pair.
  • This creates huge lone pair–bond and lone pair–lone pair repulsion at 90° angles.

This situation is much worse than cases like AX5E or AX4E2, where we can still arrange lone pairs more favorably.

5. Compare with Allowed Cases: AX₅E and AX₄E₂

✔ AX5E (one lone pair)

For AX5E, we have 5 bonds and 1 lone pair. We can place the single lone pair in one hybrid orbital, and the result is a square pyramidal shape. Repulsions are increased compared to AX6, but still manageable.

✔ AX4E2 (two lone pairs)

For AX4E2, we can place the two lone pairs in opposite sp³d² orbitals (180° apart). This minimizes lone pair–lone pair repulsion and gives a stable square planar geometry.

❌ AX3E3 (three lone pairs)

With three lone pairs:

  • It is impossible to place all three lone pairs opposite each other.
  • At least some lone pairs must be at 90° to each other and to bonds.
  • This leads to extremely strong repulsions and a very unstable arrangement.

Because all six sp³d² hybrids are equivalent, you cannot create a “special set” of orbitals just for lone pairs that avoids this repulsion.

6. What Does VBT Predict Will Happen Instead?

Since three lone pairs cannot be placed in sp³d² orbitals without extreme repulsion, Valence Bond Theory predicts that:

  • The molecule will not adopt a six-domain (octahedral) arrangement with AX3E3.
  • Instead, the system will reorganize its bonding into a different hybridization with fewer domains, such as:
    • sp³ (tetrahedral: e.g., AX4E2)
    • or sp³d (trigonal bipyramidal: e.g., AX2E3)
  • This effectively lowers the coordination number and avoids the impossible AX3E3 pattern.

7. Final Summary (VBT View)

In Valence Bond Theory:

  • An octahedral arrangement uses six equivalent sp³d² hybrid orbitals.
  • For AX3E3, we would need 3 bonds and 3 lone pairs all placed in these hybrids.
  • Because all hybrids point in specific directions and are equivalent, there is no low-repulsion way to place 3 lone pairs.
  • At least some lone pairs must sit 90° from each other and from bonds, causing severe electron–electron repulsion.
  • This makes the AX3E3 arrangement too unstable to exist as a real, stable geometry.

Therefore, VBT predicts that AX3E3 is not a stable possibility; the molecule will instead adopt a different geometry with fewer electron domains.
